# Conclusion guidance

Project and journal rules take priority. Some journals use a separate Conclusion section; others expect the final Discussion paragraph to perform this function.

## Purpose

Give the clearest supported answer to the research question and state what that answer means at the appropriate scope.

A conclusion may include four rhetorical functions:

1. **Main answer** — the principal finding or resolution of the study question.
2. **Meaning** — the scientific, practical, theoretical, or methodological implication supported by that finding.
3. **Boundary** — the main limit needed to interpret the answer accurately.
4. **Next step** — a specific recommendation, application, or future direction when it follows directly from the evidence.

These are functions, not a mandatory sentence template. Use only those the manuscript needs.

## Rules

- Synthesize rather than repeat the Results or Discussion.
- State the strongest conclusion the evidence supports, and no stronger.
- Keep only the principal boundary needed to interpret the finding; do not repeat a full limitations inventory.
- Future recommendations are optional. Include them when they are specific, consequential, and derived from the findings.
- Avoid generic endings such as “more research is needed”. Name the unresolved question, validation need, or decision that follows.
- Do not introduce new results, analyses, mechanisms, concepts, comparisons, or arguments.
- Do not introduce new literature-based claims.
- Avoid a citation appearing for the first time in the conclusion. Retain a citation only when it supports a claim already developed earlier and remains necessary in the conclusion.
- A short conclusion is appropriate when the Discussion has already completed the interpretation.
- Do not add a separate Conclusion section when it would only repeat the final Discussion paragraph and the venue does not require one.

## Citation decision

For each citation in a conclusion, ask:

1. Was the source and supported claim already developed earlier in the manuscript?
2. Is the citation still needed for the concluding sentence to be accurate?
3. Would moving the literature-based claim into the Discussion make the conclusion cleaner?

If the answer to the first question is no, move or remove the new claim rather than introducing it in the conclusion.

## Flexible default

```text
main answer → supported meaning → essential boundary → justified implication or next test
```

Do not force this sequence when a shorter or differently ordered ending better fits the study and journal.

## Audit questions

- Does the conclusion answer the stated research question?
- Does it stay within the study's population, place, time, design, and analytical scope?
- Does it distinguish association, prediction, mechanism, and causation correctly?
- Does each implication follow from evidence already presented?
- Is any sentence introducing new evidence or literature?
- Are future recommendations proportionate to the study?
- Does the final sentence end on the most useful supported takeaway rather than significance puffery?
